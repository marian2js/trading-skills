# Concentration Framework

Use this framework to judge whether a portfolio is less diversified than the raw ticker count suggests.

## What concentration risk actually means

Concentration risk is not only "one giant stock position."

It can also come from:

- multiple holdings tied to the same issuer
- sector overlap
- thematic overlap
- benchmark overlap
- employer stock
- illiquid positions that cannot be reduced easily
- partial portfolio views that hide true exposures

## Source-informed heuristics

These are practical review thresholds, not universal laws:

- A single position at roughly `5%` of total portfolio value deserves review.
- A single issuer above roughly `10%` is often already an elevated concentration case for many investors.
- Employer stock above roughly `10-20%` is a notable overconcentration risk because income and portfolio value may depend on the same company.
- Two or three concentrated single-name positions can create concentration risk even if no single holding looks extreme by itself.

These heuristics are informed by retail-investor guidance and portfolio tools from FINRA, Fidelity, and Schwab. Use them as prompts for review, not mechanical rules.

## Direct vs indirect concentration

Direct concentration:

- one stock
- one bond issuer
- one narrow ETF

Indirect concentration:

- individual semiconductor names plus a semiconductor ETF
- multiple AI or cloud names with the same demand driver
- broad indexes that already have large weights in the same mega-cap holdings
- company stock plus compensation, restricted stock, or career exposure to the same issuer

## What to look for

Ask:

- What are the top three positions?
- What are the top three correlated clusters?
- If one thesis fails, how many holdings fail with it?
- Could the user reduce the exposure quickly?
- Is this the full portfolio or just one sleeve?

## Typical caution cases

- employer stock paired with salary dependence
- narrow sector ETFs layered on top of the same underlying single names
- thematic baskets that look diversified by label but share the same catalyst chain
- portfolios tied closely to cap-weighted indexes during periods of heavy mega-cap concentration
- illiquid or tax-constrained winners that grew into oversized positions

## Boundaries

This framework does not decide:

- whether the thesis is valid
- where to enter
- where the stop belongs
- how large the position should be

It only answers whether the portfolio is already too dependent on a small set of exposures.
