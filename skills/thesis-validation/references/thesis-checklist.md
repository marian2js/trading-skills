# Thesis Checklist

Use this checklist to keep a thesis specific, testable, and reusable.

## Core questions

- What exactly is the claim?
- Why should that claim be true?
- Why now?
- What timeframe does the claim belong to?
- What would weaken it?
- What would break it?

## Evidence discipline

Separate three buckets clearly:

- `facts`: what is actually observed in the user's notes, filings, earnings, tape, or macro context
- `inference`: what those facts may imply
- `assumptions`: what still has to be true for the thesis to work

If the thesis depends mostly on assumptions, it is not ready for trade construction.

## Timeframe fit

- Short-horizon trade theses should lean on near-term catalysts, tape behavior, regime, and timing.
- Medium-horizon swing theses should connect the idea to a realistic catalyst path and a manageable dependency set.
- Long-horizon investment theses should not be validated using only short-term price action.

## Invalidation quality

Good invalidation is concrete:

- a thesis-critical data point fails
- a key catalyst delivers the wrong read-through
- leadership, breadth, or macro conditions change enough to break the logic
- management guidance, demand, margins, or policy backdrop invalidates the core claim

Bad invalidation is vague:

- "the market disagrees"
- "I lose confidence"
- "it stops working"

## Boundaries

This checklist is for thesis quality only.

It does not answer:

- where to enter
- where to place a stop
- how large to trade
- whether the portfolio can absorb the risk

Those belong to downstream skills.
